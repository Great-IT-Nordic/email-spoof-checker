📚What are SPF, DKIM, DMARC and MX records? Learn
🛡️ SPF — Sender Policy Framework

What it does: SPF is a DNS TXT record listing which servers are authorized to send email on behalf of your domain. Receiving servers check the sending IP against this list.

v=spf1 include:spf.protection.outlook.com -all

The all policy is critical:

  • -all Hard fail — unauthorized servers rejected. Best.
  • ~all Soft fail — accepted but flagged as suspicious.
  • +all Pass all — everyone allowed. Never use this.
  • ?all Neutral — no protection.

Without SPF: anyone can send email from your domain with no DNS check failing.

🔑 DKIM — DomainKeys Identified Mail

What it does: DKIM adds a cryptographic signature to outgoing email. The receiving server verifies it using a public key in DNS. Tampering breaks the signature.

selector1._domainkey.contoso.com TXT "v=DKIM1; k=rsa; p=MIGf..."
  • Selector — a label allowing multiple keys (Exchange uses selector1/selector2, Google uses google)
  • Signing — private key signs emails; only your server holds it
  • Verification — receiver fetches public key from DNS and checks the signature

Without DKIM: emails can be forged or altered in transit without detection.

🏛️ DMARC — Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance

What it does: DMARC tells receivers what to do with email that fails SPF/DKIM. It also enables reporting — you receive XML reports showing who sends on your behalf.

_dmarc.contoso.com TXT "v=DMARC1; p=reject; rua=mailto:dmarc@contoso.com"
  • p=none Monitor only — failing emails still delivered
  • p=quarantine Failing mail → spam/junk folder
  • p=reject Failing mail blocked outright (goal)

Path: none → quarantine → reject. Without DMARC there is no enforcement regardless of SPF/DKIM.

📬 MX — Mail Exchanger Records

What it does: MX records tell the world where to deliver inbound email for your domain.

contoso.com MX 10 contoso-com.mail.protection.outlook.com
  • Exchange Online: ends in .mail.protection.outlook.com
  • Google Workspace: aspmx.l.google.com
  • No MX = domain can\'t receive email — but CAN still be used in a spoofed From address

Non-sending domains should still publish v=spf1 -all and DMARC p=reject to block abuse of their name.

📋How to extract email headers from Outlook, Gmail & others Learn
🖥️ Outlook Desktop (Windows)
  1. Open the email.
  2. Go to File → Properties (or press Alt+Enter).
  3. Scroll to the Internet headers box at the bottom.
  4. Click inside → Ctrl+A → Ctrl+C.
  5. Paste into the Header Analysis tab.

New Outlook for Windows: open email in a new window first (double-click), then File → Properties.

🌐 Outlook Web Access (OWA)
  1. Open the email in OWA.
  2. Click ⋯ (three dots) top-right of the email.
  3. Select "View message source" or "View message details".
  4. Copy the headers section (everything before the first blank line).
📧 Gmail
  1. Open the email in Gmail.
  2. Click ⋮ (three dots) next to the Reply button.
  3. Select "Show original".
  4. Click "Copy to clipboard" at the top of the new tab.
  5. Paste into the Header Analysis tab.

Gmail's Show original page also has a built-in SPF/DKIM/DMARC summary at the top.

🍎 Apple Mail
  1. Open the email.
  2. Menu: View → Message → All Headers (or Shift+Cmd+H).
  3. Select and copy all header text.
  4. Paste into the Header Analysis tab.
🚨What to look for in a spoofed or phishing email Learn

Common warning signs — you do not need to be technical to spot most of these.

🔍 From address looks right but isn\'t

Lookalike domains: contoso.com vs contoso-secure.com. Always reveal the actual email address behind the display name.

↩️ Reply goes to a different domain

The From says hr@contoso.com but Reply-To is payroll@gmail.com. Replies go to the attacker. This tool checks for this automatically.

⚡ Manufactured urgency

"Your account suspends in 24h", "Urgent CEO request". Pressure to act fast without thinking is one of the most reliable phishing signals.

🔗 Links don't go where they say

Hover before clicking. Display text says microsoft.com, real link goes to micr0soft-login.ru. On mobile, long-press to preview.

📎 Unexpected attachments

Especially .zip, .iso, .html, .docm, .xlsm. Verify by phone before opening — even from known senders if the context is odd.

🏦 Requests for money or credentials

Verify any request to change bank details, wire money, or share passwords by phone — using a number you already know, not one from the email.

✉️ Business email from free provider

Legitimate businesses use their own domain. Email from @gmail.com or @hotmail.com claiming to be a supplier is always worth scrutinising.

🔐 Authentication failures in headers

SPF, DKIM, or DMARC failing in Authentication-Results is the strongest technical indicator of spoofing. Exactly what this tool checks.

💡 Quick checklist before acting on a suspicious email
  1. Does the email address exactly match the claimed company domain?
  2. Was this email expected? Did anyone tell you it was coming?
  3. Does the request make sense for this person's role?
  4. Is there urgency or pressure to act without verifying?
  5. Can you confirm by phone (using a known number) if money or credentials are involved?
